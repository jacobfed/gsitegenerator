Quantifying BOS in the Presence of Infection


Pulmonary complications, both infectious and noninfectious, are a common cause of morbidity and mortality after hematopoietic cell transplantation (HCT). Within this context, bronchiolitis obliterans syndrome (BOS) remains particularly problematic. Once present, the prognosis of affected patients is poor, with 5-year overall survival <20%. This is attributed to late diagnosis of BOS partly because infection must be absent. We have modified our PRM approach to quantify BOS in the presence of pulmonary infections.

Monitor BOS Progression using PRM


Long-term survival after lung transplantation (LTx) is hampered by chronic lung allograft dysfunction (CLAD), which is a consequence of graft-vs-host disease. With one of the highest rejection rates among all solid organ transplantations, approximately 50% of LTx recipients suffer from CLAD within 5 years after transplantation. We evaluated PRM as a method, complementary to pulmonary function tests, to monitor BOS progression.

Predicting Overall Survival of BOS LTx recipients


The predominant cause of chronic lung allograft failure is small airway obstruction arising from bronchiolitis obliterans. However, clinical methodologies for evaluating presence and degree of small airway disease are lacking. PRM is a novel imaging tool for lung transplant recipients presenting with spirometric decline. Quantifying underlying mall airway obstruction via PRM helps further stratify the risk of death in patients with diverse spirometric decline patterns.
